Exploratory Data Analysis: NYC Leading Causes of Death

Authors: Eva Soboleva, Bayansulu Tulepbayeva

About the Dataset

This dataset comes from NYC Open Data and contains information about leading causes of death in New York City from 2007–2021.

The data includes number of deaths, death rates, age-adjusted death rates, sex, race/ethnicity, and cause of death.

Potential Biases: Data may reflect disparities in healthcare access, reporting inconsistencies, racial classification inconsistencies (e.g., "Black Non-Hispanic" vs "Non-Hispanic Black"), and underreporting in certain populations.

Data Distributions

Distribution of Deaths

The histogram of total deaths shows a strong right-skewed distribution. Most causes of death have relatively low death counts, while a small number of causes have extremely high totals. This pattern explains why the mean (602.96) is much higher than the median (220). The skew suggests that mortality in NYC is concentrated among a few dominant causes.

Death Rate Distribution

The distribution of death rates mirrors the pattern seen in total deaths. Most causes have relatively low rates, but several causes show very high mortality rates. The long right tail supports the large standard deviation and wide range observed in the summary statistics.

Records by Year

The number of records appears relatively consistent across years from 2007 to 2021. This suggests that the dataset provides stable annual coverage, allowing reliable trend analysis over time.

Age-Adjusted Death Rate (Boxplot)

The boxplot shows substantial variability in age-adjusted death rates, with several high outliers. This confirms that certain causes are disproportionately deadly even after adjusting for age differences across populations.

Counts by Sex

The dataset includes nearly equal representation of male and female records, enabling meaningful comparison between sexes in later analyses.

Counts by Race/Ethnicity

The racial and ethnic distribution shows uneven representation across categories. Additionally, inconsistencies in labeling (e.g., similar groups listed separately) highlight potential classification bias within the dataset.

Relationships in the Data

Top 10 Leading Causes of Death

The bar chart of the top 10 leading causes shows that Diseases of the Heart and Malignant Neoplasms (Cancer) account for significantly more deaths than other causes. The large gap between these and the remaining categories supports the hypothesis that heart disease is the dominant cause of mortality in NYC.

Average Death Rate Over Time

The line chart shows a noticeable spike in average death rate around 2020–2021. This increase suggests a significant shift in mortality during this period, likely influenced by the COVID-19 pandemic. This pattern directly informed the hypothesis predicting increased mortality during these years.

Top 10 Causes by Sex

Comparing the top 10 causes by sex reveals differences in mortality patterns between males and females. In several major categories, male death totals appear higher, suggesting potential sex-based disparities in mortality. This visualization supports the hypothesis that mortality differs by sex across major diseases.

Testable Hypotheses

Hypothesis 1

Diseases of the Heart account for significantly more deaths than other leading causes in New York City.

This hypothesis was informed by the visualization of the top 10 leading causes of death. The bar chart shows a clear gap between Diseases of the Heart and most other causes, indicating that heart disease consistently ranks as one of the dominant contributors to mortality. Because the difference in total deaths appears substantial, this hypothesis can be tested statistically by comparing mean death counts across causes.

Hypothesis 2

Death rates increased significantly during 2020–2021 compared to previous years.

The line chart of average death rate over time shows a noticeable spike around 2020–2021. This visual trend suggests a significant shift in mortality during those years. Given the historical context of the COVID-19 pandemic, this increase appears meaningful and testable by comparing average death rates before and during this period.

Hypothesis 3

In the majority of leading causes of death, females experience higher total deaths than males.

This hypothesis is based on the grouped bar chart comparing the top 10 leading causes of death by sex. In several categories, female death totals appear higher than male totals. This pattern suggests a possible overall trend in which females experience greater mortality counts across diseases. This hypothesis can be tested by comparing total deaths by sex across all leading causes.
